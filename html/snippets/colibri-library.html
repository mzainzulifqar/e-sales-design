Accountability is the act of choosing to take personal ownership for something. It is fundamental to high performing individuals and teams. When reviewing the past, it involves looking at your role in events to see how you have played a part in the outcome. It's about understanding what you did or didn't do that was essential to the outcome and how your actions influenced events.

Taking accountability is a commitment to self-awareness. It involves reviewing any assessments or judgments you may have made or actions you have taken that could have influenced a given outcome. This self-knowledge broadens your range of options for response in the future and allows you to influence outcomes in a more positive way.

Accountability is a personal choice about how to approach life and every situation it presents. It is not the same as responsibility, which can be delegated or assigned. Accountability occurs only by choice.

Breakthrough Learning is a process that involves identifying and observing current belief systems, getting curious to determine which of these systems support or hinder progress, and then making a shift to open new possibilities and outcomes.

This process is designed to break belief systems and achieve significant learning, as opposed to incremental learning. It involves moving out of one's comfort zone and into the learning zone, balancing between analysis and learning, and acting and learning fast.

The goal is to see new possibilities, take new actions, and create individual and collective new practices to support the desired future. This approach to learning requires active participation and engagement, and it emphasizes the importance of practice, as learning involves not just taking in the new, but undoing the old.


The Cycle of Leadership is a framework that outlines three critical areas for taking effective action:

Conversations for Possibilities: This involves creating an environment where the best ideas are brought forward and considered. This stage is key for considering new possibilities and ways of doing things.

Declarations: Once the best ideas are on the table, leaders are in a position to make declarations, defining a new future state. With clear declarations, organizations can move forward with purpose and pace.

Conversations for Results: With a clear direction in place, people are now in a position to execute. The clearer and more precise these exchanges are, the more accurate and effective the downstream execution will be.

The Cycle of Leadership is a continuous process that can be replicated throughout an organization. It emphasizes the importance of open and honest communication, and the role of the leader in ensuring these dynamics exist throughout the organization. The leader's role is to ensure that the necessary conversations are occurring, and that the essential team commitments are made and fulfilled.

Filter in this context refers to our personal interpretation of reality, influenced by our life experiences, beliefs, and emotions. This filter impacts our actions, decisions, and relationships. It helps us make sense of the world, focus on what's important to us, and keep us safe. However, it can also limit our perception and understanding if we're not aware of its function.

Our filter is constantly at work, deciding what information is relevant and what isn't. It can create our interpretation of reality, but it can also confuse our reality. It can limit our performance and potential if we don't take the time to understand it and the filters of others.

Our filter is influenced by both our conscious and unconscious mind. We are constantly taking in a high influx of data, but we can only focus on a small amount each moment. Our mind decides which ones are relevant.

Every person's filter is different and these differing points of view can create conflict, friction, and frustration. If we're not aware of how our filter works, it can prevent us from collaborating, sharing, being vulnerable, reaching our potential, and innovating.

Our life experience and our amygdala, the part of our brain responsible for our emotions, survival instincts, and memory, have a direct impact on our filter. Our filter can also be influenced by our beliefs, which act as filters on what we see as possible.


Centering is a practice that involves becoming open, present, and connected. It's about achieving a state of wholeness in which effective action, emotional security, mental alertness, and spiritual connection are in balance. When you are centered, you find confidence in your ability to influence others and grow your competence in your chosen field.

There are three key distinctions when developing a centering practice:

Presence: This means being in the moment and bringing awareness to yourself. This can be achieved by bringing attention and awareness to your body, developing slow, deep breathing, relaxing your muscles, and quieting any clenching or twitching.

Openness: Becoming present in your physical self allows you to open up your nervous system and senses to incoming stimuli in a non-judgmental way.

Connection: This involves being emotionally connected, mentally aware, and spiritually aligned with a purpose beyond your immediate self-interest.

Centering practices can include breathing and stretching techniques for releasing tension, increasing awareness, and managing stress. These practices can help you stay calm, relaxed, and alert at the same time, which is useful for both stress management and maintaining clear, focused thinking/action under pressure.

Practices are activities that you do regularly, often daily or several times a week, to produce valuable changes. They are crucial to shifting who you are and are always ongoing. The more an action is repeated, the more likely it is to become embodied, meaning it becomes a part of who you are that you no longer have to think about.

Practices are characterized by being hard work and pushing you out of your comfort zone into your learning zone. They take you to the edge of your ability, pushing you to perform a little better than you are currently able. As you improve, the bar must be continually raised to solidify newly developed capabilities and push for the next level.

Effective practices have six key elements:

  1. Be in choice: You must choose to commit to changing your behavior.
  2. Have clarity on the capability or outcome you desire.
  3. Be clear on why the outcome is important.
  4. The practice needs to take you to your edge or outside your comfort zone.
  5. It needs to be intentional and regular.
  6. Create a community: Practices are often best done with one or more partners, and having a teacher, coach, or mentor can be beneficial.

Practices are not just habits. While habits reinforce existing neural pathways, practices develop new ones. They require a willingness to do something different, to give up old ways of being, examine beliefs, and let go of what is no longer serving you.

Gratitude is considered the "ultimate performance enhancing" according to psychology professor Dr. Robert A. Emmons. It involves three types: Respect, Appreciation, and Generosity.

  • Respect refers to having regard for yourself, the environment, and the people around you.
  • Appreciation involves acknowledging the good in your thoughts and voicing it aloud.
  • Generosity is about giving of your time, attention, and resources.

Practicing gratitude can increase joy and the quality of our relationships, and guide us to a more fulfilling future.

There are two main practices of gratitude mentioned in the context.

The first practice, to be done at least three days per week, involves taking five minutes to write down what you are grateful for and why it is important to you.

The second practice, to be done at least two days per week, involves telling someone what you appreciate about them or acknowledging something positive they are doing and why that is valuable.


The six Performance Principles form the foundation of high performance. These are:

  • Awareness: Being conscious of, and accurately perceiving, what is happening in yourself and in the world, and in the interaction between the two.
  • Choice: Centered in Awareness. You can only make choices about things you are aware of.
  • Accountability: Choosing to be accountable for something bigger than yourself and understanding your part in the outcome.
  • Honesty: Being truthful and transparent, revealing more of yourself to increase honesty.
  • Integrity: Ensuring what you think, say, and do are consistent.
  • Trust: Assuming good intent until proven otherwise, being vulnerable, supporting each other, fulfilling commitments, and managing promises.

These principles are practiced both individually and collectively to establish a leadership culture. They require effort, attention, and intention to change usual patterns of behavior. High performance, particularly sustainable high performance, inevitably requires effort and practice.

Assertions and Assessments are two different types of statements.

Assertions are statements of fact that can be proven true or false with demonstrable evidence. They reflect the external world and deal primarily with the past and the present. Assertions are largely generated in the intellect, as a result of measurement and analysis.

On the other hand, assessments are statements of interpretation or opinion. They cannot be proven true or false. Assessments reflect the internal world, our emotions and beliefs. They are relevant to the future as we make them in order to shape our actions to create a desirable future. Assessments are largely generated in the emotional brain, which operates about 30 times as fast as the intellectual brain, and is wired to assume the worst.

While assessments cannot be proven true or false, it is possible to provide grounding for them. Grounding assessments means providing the reason for holding the assessment. This can be done through concern for the future, the context in which the assessment is being held, supporting assertions, and standards.


Grounding Assessments is a vital aspect of high-performance communication and collaboration. It involves providing clear reasons for your assessment and evaluating its usefulness.

Often, conversations revolve around differing assessments rather than addressing the underlying concerns. Grounding an assessment can be achieved through several methods:

  1. Identifying the concern that prompted the assessment
  2. Specifying the domain or context
  3. Highlighting the supporting assertions or facts
  4. Establishing the shared or agreed-upon standards.

Grounding assessments enhances communication and decision-making, instilling confidence in your decisions. The validity of an assessment is gauged by your readiness to scrutinize the reasons behind it and to reflect on your beliefs and patterns of holding your assessments as "truth".

Mastering the art of assessments, which includes managing your own and responding to others', is a lifelong endeavor that can significantly improve relationships and effectiveness in various roles.
